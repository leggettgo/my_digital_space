The Asterix comic series was a staple bedtime and bathroom read of my early childhood. I look back on the racist depictions with distaste (more on this here), however the word play in the character names is a treasure of the stories that sticks with me still. Getafix the druid, Unhygienix the fishmonger, and Cacofonix the ill-talented bard all paled in comparison to my favourite animal companion Dogmatix, an orbital and subtly pivotal character in each story. This English translation is an even more fitting title than the original French name Id√©fix, which plays on the expression id√©e fixe (fixed idea).

Far from the quirky, loyal and stubborn character in those stories, dogmatism itself is a trait I find worrying and far too easily adopted. It underlies the principles of conservatism - keeping things the same because they have been kept the same - an ideology that stifles progress and upholds many violent social hierarchies. As an individual it is also easy to be held back by your own deep rooted beliefs.

A profound change I made at the round age of 20 was to follow a vegan lifestyle. The decision came from a slew of research into food systems, climate change, and animal ethics. Why, then, did I have such a strong opinion about the importance of consuming meat prior to this, when I had done nothing to inform that behaviour? Looking back it is easy to see the impact of British cultural normalisation and a pressure to conform to masculine stereotypes. Upbringing and environment were my biggest enablers.

Going vegan felt incredibly liberating. I had an informed and renewed autonomy over a huge aspect of my lifestyle, and knew that my choices benefited other earthlings. I found much more joy in food and my cooking improved dramatically as I experimented with new and complex plant based recipes. However, I increasingly started to see the topic as black and white. I struggled to understand why everyone couldn't peek behind the proverbial curtain and make the change themselves. Relationships were put under strain and social scenarios around food became awkward. The dogma had returned.

The years since then have given way to more important perspective shifts, and each learning experience has emphasised the nuance of the previous. I am still an ethical vegan and hold many strong beliefs that shape my life, but I have slowly removed the sense of finality around them. For veganism this involves the understanding that morality is subjective (although I do think that most people share a similar moral framework and it is largely ignorance that does not move the needle on this issue). It is important to me that the ideas I give weight to do not crush my critical consideration of new and conflicting information. I want to learn and I want to change, because I know the world is not static.
