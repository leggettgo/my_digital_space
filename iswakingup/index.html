I've been reading a lot of science fiction recently. Whether it's the interstellar world building of space operas like the Dune or Foundation series, or the advent of advanced biotech in Dogs of War, the genre remains a beautiful source of inspiration and wonder for me. Seeing that the fields of robotics, telecommunications, and space travel have all been significantly influenced by concepts great sci-fi writers have dreamt up tells me that I'm not alone in my fascination.

Last week, in an effort to redirect my reading from awe and imagination to some serious stone cold learning, I picked up Women, Race & Class by Angela Davis. Ten pages into the visceral account of the enslaved black woman's experience in 18th Century American plantations, a familiar chorus of upset, anger, and disgust rose up in me. That same week, I participated in local actions for Palestine, set in motion the start of a political book club with a colleague, and spent a lot of energy exploring these amped up feelings to those close to me who I know share similar ideologies.

This experience was not new. I remember it from attending a screening of Land of Hope and Glory, whilst reading Akala's Natives: Race and Class in the Ruins of Empire, and in the days following October 7th, 2023. All of these moments led me to dial up my knowledge of and engagement with current political issues. That week was the most politically focused I have been since my arrival in Amsterdam nine months ago, even though I had the intention of at a minimum participating in local activist projects here. So why, since my perspective on these causes has not changed, do I let my attention fade away?

Reflecting on my life during these months, I can identify a few systems that are invested in my political disengagement.

Capitalist Fatigue
I am not alone in working a physically and mentally demanding job. I can often be stretched for time in the mornings and drained in the evenings. I prioritise sleep in order to work well, and fill much of my free time with life admin that I can't take care of during the nine-to-five. This does an incredible job of keeping me politically inert. Where is the time or energy to focus on societal issues around this work on which I rely to live?

Individualism
An unspoken encouragement to put my myself first. I am rewarded with social and economic capital on my personal wins, which can largely revolve around my position, responsibilities, and salary. Even during the time I spent carefully considering my career path, my persistent job search was underlined with a subconscious competitive desire to rise above my imaginary rival applicants. Meanwhile, I have seen collective action acknowledged and praised far less than someone who has (against the odds or not) become financially successful in their own right.

Sedative Comfort
I like my treats, goddammit. Watching films, eating good food, visiting new places, buying that matt black temperature controlled gooseneck kettle I've always wanted. My desire for material comfort is strong, and when I have this I am left with a convincing sense of security and fulfilment. Even with intentional gestures of ethical consumption and donations to nonprofits I can be found under the convincing spell of 'doing enough'. This keeps me at a suitable separation from exploring the discomfort and unrest that is necessary to enact meaningful change in the world.

So, I have awoken from this slumber of inaction. That's awesome, and I will strive to put my energy to good use. But these sneaky forces are patiently waiting to bring me back under, aren‚Äôt they? The cycle of learning, feeling, forgetting, and returning might be inevitable, but I can certainly put in place some habits to help keep this proverbial flame alive. The importance of exposing myself to emotional catalysts* like Women, Race and Class is apparent. As is the benefit of having accountability buddies around me who I can discuss these feelings and participate in actions with. If those fail, I am also writing on this experience to keep a neat record that I will be prompted to reflect on when I start a new post on Gaming the Career Ladder and Retiring Early in a couple of years' time (please no).
